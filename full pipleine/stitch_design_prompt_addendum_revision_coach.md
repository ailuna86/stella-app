# Stitch prompt addendum — Essay Revision + Writing Coach

Paste this after screen 12 in your existing `stitch_design_prompt.md` prompt (or as a follow-up message in the same Stitch project so it inherits the design system already established). It uses the exact same design system — colors, card style, button shapes, typography — defined at the top of that original brief, so don't restate it; just reference it.

---

13. **Essay revision workspace (Gold only, two states on the same screen).**

    This screen has two states depending on whether the student has submitted their own revision yet. Both states use the same page shell: a header showing word count and a quick summary ("X sentences to rewrite, Y to check"), and a short intro line explaining what the screen is for — students land here from a "Revise this essay" button on their feedback report, and many will be new to the concept, so don't assume they know what a revision workspace is.

    **State A — before self-revision (default).** The original essay is shown broken into paragraph cards in reading order. Each paragraph card is bordered and tinted green, amber/yellow, or rose depending on its status (green = keep as is, yellow = improve or double-check, red = rewrite), labeled with its role (Introduction / Body / Conclusion) and a short plain-language note on how well it's doing its job (e.g. "Use the introduction to frame the task, state the position, and preview the main arguments"). Inside each paragraph card, individual sentences are shown as their own smaller colored chips or rows using the same three-color system, each with a one-line hint under it explaining what to fix (e.g. "This sentence has a verb form issue to fix before you keep it"). A "Wave 1" priority banner sits near the top in amber, telling the student which sentences to check first. Below the paragraph list, a checklist card ("Before you resubmit") lists a handful of concrete things to confirm. At the very bottom, a large text area pre-filled with the student's original essay text lets them write their revision directly, with a word counter and a primary "Resubmit for re-evaluation" button — full-pipeline resubmission that produces a brand new band score, so make it clear this uses one of their evaluation credits, shown as a small counter near the button.

    **State B — after self-revision (AI comparison, unlocked).** Once the student has resubmited their own revision, this state becomes available (a locked/unlocked toggle or a second tab, "Compare with AI model," makes the state change legible — don't just silently swap content). A short explanatory banner at the top clarifies the important nuance: "This model rewrite is generated from your *original* essay, not your revision — use it to compare both against a strong version. Do not copy it as your own work." For each paragraph, show a three-column comparison card (stacked vertically on mobile): **Original** | **Your revision** | **AI model rewrite**, each in its own light-bordered box, with the AI model column tinted mint to distinguish it as the "target" reference. Under each paragraph's three columns, a small collapsible note lists 2-3 short "Why this is stronger" bullets, and — where available — a "Useful lexical upgrades" expandable list showing `original phrase → stronger phrase` pairs with a one-line reason each. At the bottom, an optional collapsed "Show full AI model essay" reveals the complete model essay as continuous text when expanded, with a persistent small reminder not to copy it verbatim.

14. **Writing coach mission (Gold only, two states).**

    Reached from a "Writing coach" teaser card on the Gold dashboard (screen 7) and/or a "Coach" nav item. Framed as a short daily exercise, not a test — keep the tone encouraging and low-stakes throughout, matching the app's overall voice.

    **State A — today's mission.** A highlighted header card (violet background, white text, matching the Gold-tier card style already used elsewhere) shows the mission title, a one-line description of today's focus skill, the weekly focus label, a streak goal ("Complete 3 missions this week"), and an estimated time badge (e.g. "10 min"). Below it, the mission detail card shows: the specific goal for today in plain language, a short numbered list of steps to follow, a set of "stimulus" prompts the student responds to — each shown as its own small numbered card (e.g. "1. solar power / cheaper / coal (over the past decade)") — and a compact checklist ("Before you submit, check...") reinforcing the 2-3 things that matter most for this skill. Below that, a response text area where the student writes one answer per numbered item, with light placeholder guidance showing the expected format, and a primary "Submit for grading" button. Unlike essay evaluation, mission grading is fast (seconds) and unlimited, so no evaluation-credit counter here — the framing should feel closer to a quick quiz than an essay submission.

    **State B — graded result.** Replaces or slides in below the response area once graded. A status badge at the top reads Pass (mint), Partial pass (amber), or Not yet (rose), followed by a short plain-language overall comment. Two short lists follow: "What went well" (mint-tinted, checkmark bullets) and "Fix first" (rose-tinted, bullet list) — keep both short, 2-4 items max, so it doesn't read like a wall of feedback. Below that, each submitted item gets its own small card showing the student's sentence, a short explanation of the issue (if any), and — where available — a suggested revision in a mint-bordered callout. A closing instruction line tells the student what to do next (repeat the same mission, move to the next skill, etc.), followed by a "Try again" secondary button that clears the form for another attempt.

**Design consistency note for both screens:** reuse the exact green/amber(warning)/rose three-color status system consistently — it should feel like the same visual language a student already learned from the feedback report screen (screen 9), not a new color convention to learn.
